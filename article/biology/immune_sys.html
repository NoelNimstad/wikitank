;### Defenition ; The immune system is a complex network of organs, cells and proteins that defends the body against infection, whilst protecting the body's own cells. The immune system keeps a record of every germ (microbe) it has ever defeated so it can recognise and destroy the microbe quickly if it enters the body again. --- ;### Structure ; This protection is called immunity. Humans have three types of immunity - innate, adaptive, and nonspecific: ;*[lml] ;- Nonspecific immunity: Nonspecific (or passive) immunity is "borrowed" from another source and it lasts for a short time. For example, antibodies in a mother's breast milk give a baby temporary immunity to diseases the mother has been exposed to. Nonspecific External Barriers: skin, mucous membranes.
......................↓↓↓↓↓...................... ;- Innate immunity: Everyone is born with innate (or natural) immunity, a type of general protection. For example, the skin acts as a barrier to block germs from entering the body. And the immune system recognizes when certain invaders are foreign and could be dangerous. Innate Imune Response: phagocytic and natural killer cells, inflammation, fever.
......................↓↓↓↓↓...................... ;- Adaptive immunity: Adaptive (or active) immunity develops throughout our lives. We develop adaptive immunity when we're exposed to diseases or when we're immunized against them with vaccines. Adaptive Imune Response: cell-mediated immunity, humoral immunity. ;** Organs: ;*[lml] ;- Primary lymphoid organs: the bone marrow and the thymus. They create special immune system cells called lymphocytes. ;- Secondary lymphoid organs: the lymph nodes, the spleen, the tonsils and certain tissue in various mucous membrane layers in the body (for instance in the bowel). ;**

--- ;### Inflamation ;*[lml] ;- Inflammation is signaled by mast cells, which release histamine. ;- Histamine causes fluids to collect around an injury to dilute toxins. This causes swelling. ;- The temperature of the tissues may rise, which can kill temperature-sensitive microbes. ;** --- ;### Fever ;*.[lml] ;- Fever is a defense mechanism that can destroy many types of microbes. ;- While high fevers can be dangerous, some doctors recommend letting low fevers run their course without taking aspirin or ibuprofen. ;** --- ;### Specific defenses ;*.[lml] ;- Specific defenses are those that give us immunity to certain diseases. ;- In specific defenses, the immune system forms a chemical “memory” of the invading microbe. If the microbe is encountered again, the body reacts so quickly that few or no symptoms are felt. ;** --- ;### Major players ; The major players in the immune system include: ;*.[lml] ;- Macrophage - a type of white blood cell that surrounds and kills microorganisms, removes dead cells, and stimulates the action of other immune system cells. ;- T cells (helper, cytotoxic, memory) - a type of white blood cell, direct fighters of foreign invaders and also produced cytokines, which are biological substances that help activate other parts of the immune system. ;- B cells (plasma, memory) - a type of white blood cell that produce antibody molecules that can latch on and destroy invading viruses or bacteria. ;- Antibody - a protein produced by the human immune system to tag and destroy invasive microbes. ;- Antibiotic - various chemicals produced that is toxic to many bacteria. Some we use as medicins. ;- Antigen: ;*[lml] ;- - Cells of the immune system are "trained" to recognize "self" proteins vs. "not self" proteins. ;- - If an antigen ("not self") protein is encountered by a macrophage, it will bring the protein to a helper T-cell for identification. ;- - If the helper T-cell recognizes the protein as a "not self", it wall launch an immune response. ;** ;** --- ;### Helper T cells ;*.[lml] ;- Helper T-cells have receptors for recognizing antigens. If they are presented with an antigen, they release cytokines to stimulate B-cell division. ;- The helper T-cell is the key cell to signal an immune response. If helper T-cells are disabled, as they are in people with HIV, the immune system will not respond. ;** --- ;### B cells ;*.[lml] ;- B-cells in general produce antibodies. Those with antibodies that bind with the invader's antigen are stimulated to reproduce rapidly. ;- B-cells differentiate into either plasma cells or memory B-cells. Plasma cells rapidly produce antibodies. Memory cells retain the "memory" of the invader and remain ready to divide rapidly if an invasion occurs. ;** --- ;### Role of antibodies ;*.[lml] ;- Antibodies released into the blood stream will bind to antigens that they are specific for. ;- Antibodies may disable some microbes, or cause them to stick together. They "tag" microbes so that the microbes are quickly recognized by various white blood cells. ;** --- ;### Calling a halt ;*.[lml] ;- When the invader is destroyed, the helper T-cell calls a halt to the immune response. ;- Memory T-cells are formed, which can quickly divide and produce cytotoxic T-cells to quickly fight off the invader if it is ever encountered again. ;** --- ;### Helping the immune system ;*.[lml] ;- TODO ;** --- ;### How antibiotics work ;*.[lml] ;- Antibiotics help destroy bacteria (but not viruses). ;- Antibiotics work in one of several ways: ;* ;- Slowing bacteria reproduction ;- Interfering with bacteria cell wall formation. ;** ;** --- ;### Antibiotic myths ;*.[lml] ;- Antibiotics are not antibodies. ;- Antibiotics do not weaken our immune system. They help it by weakening the bacteria. ;- Humans do not become "immune" to antibiotics. Bacteria that resist antibiotics and are not completely destroyed may multiply, producing more antibiotic-resistant bacteria. ;** --- ;### Vaccine history ;*.[lml] ;- Vaccination: (from vacca, Latin for cow) Invented by Edward Jenner in 1796. Jenner knew that dairy maids who had contracted cowpox never got smallpox. He inoculated a boy with secretions from cowpox sores, and showed the boy was immune to smallpox. ;** --- ;### How vaccines work ;*.[lml] ;- Modern vaccines are created from killed bacteria or viruses, or fragments of proteins from these microbes. ;- The proteins are recognized as antigens by our immune systems. This causes a mild immune response. Memory T-cells and B-cells remain ready to fight off the illness if it is encountered again. ;** --- ;### Vaccine myths ;*.[lml] ;- The flu vaccine does not give you the flu. Some people get the vaccine too late, or catch a cold and think they have the flu. ;- Vaccines are not less effective than a "natural" infection with the illness. The immunity is the same, and a mild response to a vaccine is much less risky than a full blown response from a real virus. ;- Vaccines do not cause autism. ;** --- ;### Stomach flu? ;*.[lml] ;- Influenza is a respiratory virus. Strictly speaking, there is no stomach flu. ;- There are, however, viruses that attack the digestive system. Noro viruses and rotavirus cause the nause, vomiting and diarrhea that many people call "stomach flu". ;- Bacterial food poisoning can also cause fast-onset vomiting and diarrhea. ;** --- ;### Allergies ;*.[lml] ;- Allergies are an immune system reaction to harmless antigens. ;- Some, such as pollen, may get in through the respiratory system. Fragments of food proteins may get through the digestive system. ;- The next time these proteins are encountered, the immune system attacks them. ;- Most of the symptoms are caused by histamines released by mast cells. That is why antihistamines are used to treat allergies. ;** --- ;### Autoimmune disorders ;*.[lml] ;- Autoimmune disorders occur when the immune system fails to recognize a protein as "self" amd launches an attack. ;- Multiple sclerosis, diabetes type 1 and rheumatoid arthritis are examples. None of these can be cured, but drugs can help slow the progress of these diseases. ;** --- ;### Cancer ;*.[lml] ;- Cancer occurs when the mechanisms that control cell division fail, and body cells divide out of control. ;- Cytotoxic T-cells can recognize and destroy these cells. But if division is too rapid, the T-cells cannot keep up. ;- Some cancer research involves assisting cytotoxic T-cells in recognizing and destroying cancer cells. ;** --- ;### AIDS ;*.[lml] ;- AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome) is caused by an infection by the HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus), which attacks and destroys T-helper cells. Because it attacks the immune system directly, finding a vaccine has been difficult. ;- Some drugs can slow down HIV reproduction, but no cure exists yet. Prevention is still the best "cure". ;**
